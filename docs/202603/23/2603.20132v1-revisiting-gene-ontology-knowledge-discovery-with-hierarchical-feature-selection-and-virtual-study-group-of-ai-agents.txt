Abstract 

Large language models have achieved great success in multiple challenging tasks, and their capacity can be further boosted by the emerging agentic AI techniques. This new computing paradigm has already started revolutionising the traditional scientific discovery pipelines. In this work, we propose a novel agentic AI-based knowledge discovery-oriented virtual study group that aims to extract meaningful ageing-related biological knowledge considering highly ageing-related Gene Ontology terms that are selected by hierarchical feature selection methods. We investigate the performance of the proposed agentic AI framework by considering four different model organisms’ ageing-related Gene Ontology terms and validate the biological findings by reviewing existing research articles. It is found that the majority of the AI agent-generated scientific claims can be supported by existing literatures and the proposed internal mechanisms of the virtual study group also play an important role in the designed agentic AI-based knowledge discovery framework.  

> Keywords: Large language models, Agentic AI, Knowledge extraction and discovery, Ageing Biology, Gene Ontology, Hierarchical feature selection

# 1 Introduction 

The success of large language models (LLMs) and the emerging agentic AI techniques have already started rev-olutionalising the traditional scientific discovery paradigm. One of the most well-known examples is the agentic AI-based virtual lab [1], where real nanobody binders were successfully designed by AI agents autonomously. This achievement not only validated the feasibility of agentic AI-based scientistic research pipelines, but also boosted the research in exploring agentic AI’s possibilities in other research areas. As one of the core topics in scientific research, knowledge extraction and discovery play a fundamental role yet remain a major challenge in almost all research subjects like ageing biology, which aims to explore extremely complex theories involving enormously unrevealed biological mechanisms. Thanks to the efforts made by the community, new progress has been recently made by AI researchers who predict novel knowledge about ageing. For example, Alsaggaf, et al (2024) [2] developed novel contrastive learning algorithms to successfully predict novel ageing-related genes using protein-protein interaction networks, whilst Paz-Ruza, et al (2024) [3] also predicted novel dietary restriction-related genes by using positive-unlabelled learning algorithms and human gene-pathway associations information. However, the limited knowledge about ageing processes remains a bottleneck of any predictive models that need to exploit sufficient information to obtain reliable performance. Large language models have recently demonstrated strong capacity in handling complicated tasks (e.g. automatic computer programming code generation [4]), but their own shortcomings due to the well-known hallucination issues and lack of model interpretability still lead to concerns about its application in knowledge discovery tasks. The capacity of LLMs can be further enhanced by integrating the agentic AI approach 1

# 2 Background 

## 2.1 Gene Ontology Database 

Gene Ontology database [6] provides a type of universal vocabulary, i.e. Gene Ontology terms, to describe functions of genes and gene products. All GO terms are organised as a type of hierarchical structure, where GO terms that are located in higher positions denote more generic definitions and those are located in lower positions denote more specific definitions. As shown in the example GO hierarchy in Figure 1, GO:0050789 that denotes regulation of biological process is located in the highest position, while other GO terms, e.g. GO:0009894 (regulation of catabolic process), are located in lower positions, denoting more specific definitions of GO:0050789. Therefore, with considering genes and their corresponding Gene Ontology annotations, we are able to use a set of GO terms to describe those genes’ biological processes, molecular functions and cellular components. In addition, due to the hierarchical relationships between GO terms, when conducting biological knowledge interpretation, those GO terms bearing more specific definitions are more informative than the ones bearing more generic definitions. However, note that those GO terms bearing more specific definitions are usually independent to other GO terms that also denote specific definitions, leading to a challenge to discover associations between multiple GO terms bearing specific biological definitions. For example, as shown in Figure 1, GO:0009892 (negative regulation of metabolic process) and GO:0009889 (regulation of biosynthetic process) that denote two different specific biological processes have no interconnections according to the pre-defined hierarchy. Therefore, it will be difficult to find the interaction of the biological definitions denoted by those two GO terms, when some genes are simultaneously annotated by both of them. 

## 2.2 Hierarchical Feature Selection 

Hierarchical feature selection [7–15] is a type of dimensionality reduction methods that aim to remove redun-dant and irrelevant features with considering pre-defined hierarchical dependencies. Wan & Freitas firstly proposed a family of hierarchical feature selection methods including HIP (Select Hierarchical Information-Preserving features) [9], MR (Select Most Relevant features) [9] and HIP-MR (the hybrid of HIP and MR methods) [7] that were used to deal with the Gene Ontology database, where GO terms were considered as features. With the help of the proposed methods, a set of the most informative GO terms associated with ageing-related genes [16] were selected and ranked as a table, which provided a knowledge source for bio-logical interpretations. For example, as shown in Figure 1, with considering four different sets of genes that have been found as being relevant to ageing processes, hierarchical feature selection methods like HIP can 2derive a table showing the most informative GO terms for different model organisms. In terms of Caenorhab-ditis elegans , the most informative GO term is GO:0045202 (synapse), followed by the second top-ranked GO term GO:0000003 (reproduction). Note that, both GO:0045202 and GO:0000003 have the same frequencies of selection. Therefore, I used frequency in edges as a tie-breaking criterion, i.e. tree-augmented naive Bayes [17] was used to learn a tree for each instance by using the corresponding selected GO terms. Analogously, in terms of Drosophila melanogaster , the top-ranked GO terms are GO:0009055 (electron carrier activity) and GO:0005576 (extracellular region). In this work, I use the table reported in Wan & Freitas (2018) [18] that was generated by the HIP method as an example to evaluate the proposed AI agent-based virtual study group. 

## 2.3 Large Language Models for Knowledge Extraction 

Large Language Models are a type of AI intelligent system trained by using massive and complex data with advanced language model architectures and techniques. One of the most impressive properties of LLMs is the so-called knowledge emergence phenomenon [19, 20] that allows LLMs to convert multi-dimensional and multi-scale data into their capacity to conduct advanced activities, e.g. programming code generation [4, 21, 22], logical reasoning inference [23–25], and strategic planning [5, 26, 27]. Due to this unique property, LLMs have demonstrated great potential in knowledge extraction tasks. For example, Dagdelen, et al. (2024) [28] successfully adopted LLMs to extract a type of structured information consisting of entities and their relationships from raw text. Analogously, Balasubramanian, et al. (2025) [29] adopted LLMs to successfully extract knowledge that was presented as a set of features from pathology reports. Builtjes, et al. (2025) [30] also proposed a LLM-based framework for extracting information from clinical texts. However, note that, all those above works are quite different with the Gene Ontology terms-based knowledge interpretation tasks we discuss in this work. Also, due to the well-known shortcomings of existing LLMs, e.g. the hallucination issue, it is unclear whether LLMs are able to derive reliable biological knowledge that usually involves complex theories. 

## 2.4 Agentic AI for Scientific Research 

Agentic AI is a type of intelligent system that evolves from the conventional multi-agent systems [31] can autonomously conduct activities to achieve pre-defined goals. Compared with the conventional multi-agent systems, agentic AI systems are more powerful, due to the superbo capacities of large language models that are used as cores. An agentic AI system usually consists of multiple AI agents, which form a virtual team where each agent plays a role allowing them to cooperate with each other by passing information and calling different tools until the pre-defined goal is achieved. Recently, multiple agentic AI systems have been developed for scientific discovery tasks. For example, Ruan, et al. (2024) [32] developed a LLM-agent-based end-to-end chemical synthesis development platform that successfully conducts multiple chemical research steps including literature search, reaction kinetics study, and product purification. More recently, Swanson, et al. (2025) [1] proposed an AI agent-based virtual lab to successfully design nanobody binders. Within the virtual lab, multiple agents simulate human-like scientific discovery activities including group discussion, computational tools implementation and computational experiment results analysis. Analogously, Schmidgall, et al. (2025) [33] also developed an agent-based virtual lab that autonomously executes a completed research pipeline. Given a research idea, the proposed virtual lab can autonomously conduct literature review, research plan generation, programming code generation and results analysis, leading to a great decrease in research expense. It has also been found that the agentic AI paradigm can alleviate the hallucination issue. For example, Gosmar and Dahl (2025) [34] proposed a multi-layer framework, where agents in higher layers aim to remove hallucination information in the statements made by agents in lower layer. In this work, we also propose a multi-layer agent framework, which aims to exploit multiple agents to process LLM-generated biological knowledge by using a type of critical reviewing mechanisms to extract meaningful ageing-related knowledge considering certain biological processes and molecular functions.

# 4 Proposed Methods 

We propose an AI agents-based Virtual Study Group (VSG) framework, where the biological interpretations of the Gene Ontology terms selected by hierarchical feature selection methods can be extracted automatically. The proposed VSG framework is composed by several agents, which play different roles in a hierarchically organised virtual organization. In order to extract a general summary with considering all GO terms’ biological definitions, we propose a bottom-up structure that is used to coordinate the actions of multiple agents. As shown in Figure 2, in the first layer, multiple virtual junior researchers that mimic early year PhD students are in charge of investigating the associations between different GO terms and ageing processes. As we have four model organisms in this case, VSG uses four virtual junior researchers which have certain academic background on the corresponding model organims. For example, one virtual early-year PhD student (i.e. Virtual Junior Researcher A) whose academic background is mainly about worm will be assigned to conduct early investigations on the associations between certain GO terms and worm-related ageing processes. In addition, for each of the virtual junior researcher, we assign another virtual junior researcher (i.e. Virtual Junior Researcher B) whose duty is to criticise the findings discovered by Virtual Junior Researcher A. The second layer consists of four virtual senior researchers mimicking post-doctoral research associates, who are in charge of summarising the findings and the corresponding critics made by two virtual junior researchers (Virtual Junior Researcher A and Virtual Junior Researcher B) for one model organism. After obtaining the reports made by four virtual senior researchers, a virtual principal investigator that mimic an ageing professor will provide a final report with considering the findings about all four model organisms. We implement the proposed VSG system using CrewAI [35] and four different open-source large language models that are held by Ollama [36]. As shown in Figure 3, we use deepseek-r1 [37] and qwen3-vl [38] as the cores for Virtual Junior Researcher A and Virtual Junior Researcher B, respectively. gpt-oss [39] and glm-4.7-flash [40] are used as the cores for Virtual Senior Researchers and Virtual Principal Investigator, respectively. 5LLM Name Background Task

# 5 Experimental Results 

## 5.1 The majority of the claims made by virtual junior researchers are supported by known biological knowledge. 

We report the original outputs made by different AI agents and highlight any claims that can be validated by existing biological knowledge through conducting literature reviews on existing research articles published in decent academic journals. The phrases that are highlighted in green backgrounds denote the scientific claims that are supported by at least one existing research article, whilst the phrases in yellow backgrounds denote the scientific claims that are not supported by any existing research articles. In general, the generated reports by AI agents include both objective claims and subjective opinions. In terms of Virtual Junior Worm Researcher A and Virtual Junior Worm Researcher B, the majority of the sci-entific and objective claims are correct and can be validated by existing research articles. For example, Virtual Junior Worm Researcher A successfully points out that superoxide dismutase (SOD) and catalase (CAT) play a crucial role in antioxidant defense systems that are tightly associated with ageing. Virtual Junior Worm Researcher B also successfully further mentions the role of sod-3 in hypodermis in terms of age-related regula-tions. Analogously, Virtual Junior Fruit Fly Researcher A mentions the associations between electron carriers and mitochondrial function, and Virtual Junior Fruit Fly Researcher B further explains the role of UPR mt 

in ageing processes. In terms of Virtual Junior Mouse Researcher A, it successfully points out the associa-tions between laminopathies and progeria, whilst Virtual Junior Mouse Researcher B further mentions the role of nuclear instability in mouse’s ageing processes. Finally, in terms of Virtual Junior Yeast Researcher A, the well-known associations between mating pheromones and yeast ageing processes are successfully revealed, whilst Virtual Junior Yeast Researcher B further points out the role of quiescent cell metabolism, which is highly associated with yeast ageing processes. However, note that Virtual Junior Yeast Researcher B claims 6that ‘Hog1 deletion mutants maintain near-normal chronological lifespan in standard conditions’. This claim is not supported and an opposite conclusion was made by one existing research article. 

## 5.2 Contrary views between virtual junior researchers are successfully observed and senior researchers made fair points. 

In terms of the subjective opinions, the Virtual Junior Worm Researcher A, Virtual Junior Fruit Fly Researcher A, Virtual Junior Mouse Researcher A and Virtual Junior Yeast Researcher A all use a natural tone of certainty to summarise the biological knowledge findings by providing the corresponding objective evidence. On the contrary, the Virtual Junior Worm Researcher B, Virtual Junior Fruit Fly Researcher B, Virtual Junior Mouse Researcher B and Virtual Junior Yeast Researcher B all criticise the opinions made by their counterparts and also show corresponding objective evidence. For example, some commonly used terms are oversimplifies, overstates, and overgeneralizes, suggesting a type of critical context. In terms of those senior researchers for the four model organisms, all of them tend to agree with the criticisms made by the corresponding virtual junior researchers B by rephrasing the content provided by virtual junior researchers A and B. In addition, those virtual senior researchers all suggest that more research needs to be conducted to further investigate the ageing mechanisms for different model organisms. The comments made by the virtual ageing professor are also interesting. To begin with, it successfully points out that perhaps the more valuable direction would be related to the generalisability of the worm-based knowledge in terms of extending to mammals due to the complexity of the latter. Moreover, it acknowledges and reiterates the importance of Reactive Oxygen Species and their related biological pathways in ageing processes, but also points out the challenges of future research in this direction. Furthermore, it mentions that the discussions about the associations between reproduction and ageing need to be further expanded from evolutionary perspectives.

The conclusion overgeneralizes the mechanistic interplay between electron carriers and structural molecules in * Drosophila * ageing, presenting a monolithic narrative that neglects critical nuances. While mitochon-drial electron transport chain (ETC) dysfunction and ROS are well-established contributors to ageing, the assertion that reduced electron carrier activity *directly* accelerates ageing overlooks tissue-specific com-pensatory mechanisms – such as the mitochondrial unfolded protein response (UPR mt ) supported by [65] or glycolytic adaptations in fat body tissue supported by [66] – which can mitigate ROS damage in flies. Methodologically, the conclusion fails to address how these pathways are measured (e.g., electron carrier activity via spectrophotometry vs. *in vivo* imaging) or acknowledge that most ageing studies use short-lived laboratory strains (*w 1118 *) supported by [69] that lack physiological relevance to natural fly lifespan. Finally, it ignores key evidence that specific ETC components (e.g., Complex I) decline with age but do not *causally* drive ageing – demonstrated by stud-ies where Complex I inhibition extends lifespan via HIF-1 α-mediated metabolic shifts supported by [70] – undermining the proposed “direct impact” claim. This narrative risks reducing complex age-ing biology to a simplistic linear model without sufficient mechanistic or contextual specificity.

All of the GO terms investigated in this work are located in higher positions in the GO hierarchy, suggesting more generic biological definitions. However, validating those GO terms bearing more specific definitions would also be difficult, due to the lack of existing research, though the discovered knowledge might simultaneously provide new insights or hypotheses on future ageing research.
