# ABSTRACT 

Although deep research agents (DRAs) have emerged as a promising paradigm for complex information synthesis, their evaluation remains constrained by ad hoc empirical benchmarks. These heuristic approaches do not rigorously model agent behavior or adequately stress-test long-horizon synthesis and ambiguity resolution. To bridge this gap, we formalize DRA behavior through the lens of category the-ory, modeling deep research workflow as a composition of structure-preserving maps (functors). Grounded in this theoretical framework, we introduce a novel mechanism-aware benchmark with 296 questions designed to stress-test agents along four interpretable axes: traversing sequential connectivity chains, verify-ing intersections within V-structure pullbacks, imposing topological ordering on retrieved substructures, and performing ontological falsification via the Yoneda Probe. Our rigorous evaluation of 11 leading models establishes a persistently low baseline, with the state-of-the-art achieving only a 19.9% average accuracy, exposing the difficulty of formal structural stress-testing. Furthermore, our findings reveal a stark dichotomy in the current AI capabilities. While advanced deep research pipelines successfully redefine dynamic topological re-ordering and ex-hibit robust ontological verification—matching pure reasoning models in falsifying hallucinated premises—they almost universally collapse on multi-hop structural synthesis. Crucially, massive performance variance across tasks exposes a lingering reliance on brittle heuristics rather than a systemic understanding. Ultimately, this work demonstrates that while top-tier autonomous agents can now organically unify search and reasoning, achieving a generalized mastery over complex structural information remains a formidable open challenge. 1

# 1 INTRODUCTION 

The recent leap in large language models (LLMs) has sparked the rapid development of autonomous agents, with Deep Research Agents (DRAs) emerging as a practical solution for complex tasks (Wu et al., 2025). Unlike early systems confined to static knowledge recall (Wei et al., 2025; Zhou et al., 2025; Chen et al., 2025), true deep research is inherently dynamic (Nie et al., 2024). It requires an agent to actively seek out, cross-validate, and stitch together information from diverse and frequently conflicting sources (Hu et al., 2025). To provide genuine decision support, a DRA must go beyond simple retrieval; it needs to master the art of filtering noise, resolving contradictions, and synthesizing fragmented evidence into well-grounded judgments (Du et al., 2025). However, a rigorous theoretical modeling of deep research agent behavior remains conspicuously absent from the literature. Without a formal mathematical foundation, current evaluation paradigms rely heavily on purely empirical and ad-hoc designs (Xu & Peng, 2025). These heuristic benchmarks lack the systematism required to explicitly disentangle and measure an agent’s concrete capabilities, often blurring the lines between genuine reasoning, basic retrieval, and simple memorization (Shi

# 2 PRELIMINARY 

To formalize the behavior of deep research agents, we briefly introduce several foundational concepts from category theory (Mac Lane, 2013). Our presentation balances mathematical precision with intuitive interpretations tailored for machine learning. 2Definition 2.1 (Category) . A category C consists of a class of objects Ob( C) and, for any pair of objects X, Y ∈ Ob( C), a set of morphisms Hom C (X, Y ). These morphisms are equipped with a composition law: for f ∈ Hom C (X, Y ) and g ∈ Hom C (Y, Z ), their composition g ◦

f ∈ Hom C (X, Z ) is associative. Furthermore, every object X has an identity morphism id X ∈

Hom C (X, X ).Intuition in ML: A category abstracts a system of objects and their structure-preserving transforma-tions. In our context, objects naturally correspond to representation spaces, datasets, or hypothesis classes, while morphisms represent the functions, network layers, or reasoning paths that map between them. 

Definition 2.2 (Functor) . Given categories C and D, a functor F : C → D is a structure-preserving mapping that assigns each object X ∈ C to an object F (X) ∈ D , and each morphism f : X → Y

in C to a morphism F (f ) : F (X) → F (Y ) in D. A functor must preserve composition, i.e., 

F (g ◦ f ) = F (g) ◦ F (f ), and identity, i.e., F (id X ) = id F (X).Intuition in ML: Functors provide a principled way to translate between different architectural paradigms or representation spaces. They ensure that the relational structure (the morphisms) of the original space is perfectly preserved during the mapping—analogous to how parameterization or embedding functions map discrete symbolic spaces into continuous latent spaces while preserving semantic relationships. 

Definition 2.3 (Pullback) . Given an object Z and two morphisms f : X → Z and g : Y → Z, their pullback is an object P equipped with projection morphisms π1 : P → X and π2 : P → Y such that 

f ◦ π1 = g ◦ π2. Furthermore, P satisfies a universal property: for any object Q with morphisms 

u : Q → X and v : Q → Y satisfying f ◦ u = g ◦ v, there exists a unique morphism h : Q → P

such that π1 ◦ h = u and π2 ◦ h = v.Intuition in ML: The pullback computes the most general object whose images in X and Y are consistent under f and g. In the category of sets, it is given by P = {(x, y ) ∈ X × Y | f (x) = 

g(y)}. In machine learning, computing a pullback corresponds to enforcing equality constraints or performing multimodal fusion, where representations from different modalities ( X and Y ) must align in a shared abstract semantic space ( Z). 

Definition 2.4 (Limits and Colimits) . Let J be an index category and D : J → C be a diagram (a functor) in C. The limit of D, denoted lim D, is an object L ∈ C equipped with projection morphisms πj : L → D(j) that are compatible with all morphisms in D, satisfying a universal property analogous to the pullback. Dually, the colimit of D, denoted colim D, is the limit of the corresponding diagram in the opposite category Cop (where all morphism directions are reversed). Intuition in ML: Limits and colimits generalize the notions of aggregation and composition. A limit (which encompasses products and pullbacks) aggregates information by finding the maximal jointly consistent representation that satisfies multiple structural constraints across the diagram. Conversely, a colimit (which encompasses disjoint unions and pushouts) formalizes the process of "gluing" or ensembling objects together—such as merging distinct state spaces or representations along a shared subspace. 

# 3 CATEGORICAL FORMULATION OF DEEP RESEARCH AGENTS 

We formalize the end-to-end workflow of a Deep Research Agent (DRA) as a structural mapping from an abstract problem space to a structured conclusion space, fundamentally mediated by an external knowledge environment (the Web). While the empirical behaviors of large language models (LLMs) are often described using heuristic terminology, casting this transition into the language of category theory allows us to strictly define the requisite capabilities of an ideal DRA—such as task decomposition, information retrieval, and logical synthesis—through standard categorical constructs. More importantly, it provides a rigorous mathematical vocabulary to diagnose common agentic failure modes. More detailed proofs can be found in Appendix A. # 4 DESIGN OF EVALUATION 

The inherent scale and topological complexity of the real-world knowledge and logic categories pose significant challenges to the strict functoriality of an agent’s Search and Reasoning operations. To systematically quantify an agent’s capacity for structural preservation against phenomena like context truncation and hallucination, we propose a four-dimensional categorical evaluation framework: • Connectivity via Simple Chains: Evaluating the agent’s ability to perform linear, transitive reasoning across sequential information nodes. • Consistency via V-Structures (Pullbacks): Assessing the agent’s capacity to handle multi-source verification, intersection finding, and conflict resolution. • Organization via Substructure Re-ordering: Testing the agent’s skill in disentangling com-plex dependencies and restructuring chaotic information into ordered flows. • Structure Validity via the Yoneda Probe: Verifying the ontological reality of entities by probing their external relationships and behavioral definitions, rather than relying solely on internal attributes. 4.1 TYPE I: C ONNECTIVITY VIA SIMPLE CHAINS 

We ground the design of Type I tasks in the principle of Retrieval-Verification Asymmetry . Specifically, we construct queries where the forward search path (Problem → Answer) requires traversing an obscure, step-by-step trajectory, while the backward verification path (Answer → Problem) remains computationally straightforward. In this type, each node entity can be found on a single webpage link. To simulate the practical complexities of deep research, we decompose linear reasoning into three distinct sub-tasks, each targeting a specific phase of information linkage: Textual Fingerprinting, Vertical Knowledge Filtering, and Numerical Traceability. 

Identity Resolution via Textual Fingerprints (Task 1). In this task, the agent is presented with a semantically generic yet syntactically unique text snippet (e.g., a philosophical reflection on economics from a course note). It must resolve the author’s identity and cross-reference their activity on a specific platform, such as their GitHub contribution frequency on a specific UTC date. To ensure difficulty, we employ Adversarial Lexical Selection for the anchor text, purposefully injecting high-frequency terminology to maximize lexical collision in standard search indices and neutralize simple keyword matching. Furthermore, the task enforces Cross-Domain Hopping between unstructured academic content and structured commit logs. By targeting niche repositories and demanding precise 6timestamps, we ensure the query bypasses the “direct answer” features of commercial search engines, necessitating deep page parsing. 

Hierarchical Filtering in Vertical Knowledge Bases (Task 2). This task requires the agent to identify a target entity (e.g., a fund management company) that satisfies a conjunction of strict quantitative constraints across disjoint temporal windows on a single webpage (e.g., Net Asset Value at T1 and Total Shares at T2). Upon successful identification, the agent executes a secondary query regarding a dynamic attribute, such as new personnel qualifications within a narrow, recent time frame. Unlike open-domain QA, this simulates Vertical Search . The large-scale financial figures constrain the search space but strictly require Schema Awareness —the agent must know 

where to look (e.g., official regulatory disclosures) rather than just what to look for. Additionally, a 

Temporal Precision constraint using data from the immediate past prevents the model from relying on memorized parametric knowledge, forcing real-time interaction with dynamic web environments. 

Inverse Entity Resolution via Numerical Fingerprinting (Task 3). In this task, the agent receives a set of obscure numerical rankings (e.g., specific performance rankings of four different products) serving as a unique “numerical fingerprint” for a specific fund manager. The objective is to decode the manager’s identity and trace their career trajectory back to a distant historical point, such as their employer ten years prior. This design relies on Anti-Shortcut Engineering . We intentionally sample rankings from the long tail (e.g., 130th or 746th) rather than the head. While top rankings are heavily covered in news articles—providing trivial shortcuts—mid-tier rankings force the agent to query raw performance databases, demanding granular data processing. Moreover, the Temporal Multi-hop 

aspect tests the agent’s ability to maintain identity persistence across time. It links an instantaneous current state to a static historical attribute, heavily penalizing agents prone to hallucinating based on current affiliations. 4.2 TYPE II: C ONSISTENCY VIA V-S TRUCTURES (P ULLBACKS )While Type I evaluates linear connectivity, Type II evaluates the agent’s capacity to model V-structures, formally known as pullbacks in category theory. In these tasks, the agent must retrieve information from disjoint sources ( S1, S 2, . . . , S n) and synthesize them to discover latent constraints, verify intersections, or detect conflicts. The core challenge lies in the asymmetry of synthesis : while individual data points are computationally trivial to retrieve, recognizing their necessary intersection requires profound structural awareness. To systematically benchmark this capability, we design five abstract task paradigms, each instantiated through complex, real-world scenarios. 

Distributed Quantitative Synthesis (Task 4). This task requires the agent to deduce a specific metric by synthesizing sparse, interdependent quantitative constraints scattered across non-adjacent structural nodes, such as disparate temporal reports. Because the target answer does not exist verbatim on the web, this setup forces implicit equation solving . The agent must retrieve multiple disconnected documents, align their dynamic data schemas, formulate an algebraic relationship using a provided delta (e.g., a growth value), and solve for the unknown. To prevent the model from relying on memorized parametric knowledge or high-level summaries, we enforce anti-memorization via long-tail sampling (e.g., querying the 7th largest holding of a fund), thereby necessitating raw, multi-document data extraction and mathematical alignment. 

Entity Resolution under Hard-Negative Interference (Task 5). In this task, the agent must accu-rately profile a specific entity using a rigid, unique designator while navigating severe probabilistic distractors that share identical superficial features. We employ adversarial attribute injection by constructing scenarios where the target entity—defined strictly by a unique ID—is an obscure figure, whereas the “hard negative” distractor is a high-profile individual sharing the exact same name. This paradigm stress-tests the tension between parametric prior and contextual fact . It evaluates whether the agent anchors its reasoning on the deterministic pullback (faithfully matching the ID to the correct person) or hallucinates by falling back on its pre-training distribution (lazily matching the name to the famous entity). 

Implicit Boundary Condition Detection (Task 6). In this task, the agent is presented with a standard operational instruction, but the true objective is for it to autonomously discover and apply an unmentioned, domain-specific boundary condition that mathematically invalidates the instruction. 7Unlike traditional reasoning benchmarks that explicitly provide the rules, deep research requires 

meta-cognitive rule triggering —discovering the necessity of a rule in the first place. We instantiate this using a hypothetical financial trade that exceeds a statutory limit. The agent must independently realize that the action is bounded by compliance laws, retrieve real-time baseline data (e.g., net asset value), and compute the intersection to flag the latent violation. 

Cross-Domain Anomaly Intersection (Task 7). In this task, the agent receives a seemingly benign user request that covertly embeds partial, fragmented identifiers matching an entity in an external, open-world risk database. This requires a fuzzy database join . Although not explicitly instructed to perform a background check, regulatory compliance implicitly dictates it. We instantiate this by embedding the partial identity features of a fugitive into a routine customer service scenario. This creates a structural conflict between helpfulness (executing the user’s task) and systemic constraints (flagging the anomaly), rigorously testing the agent’s capacity for unprompted anomaly detection across disparate domains—a direct test of safety alignment override .

Latent Dependency Satisfiability (Task 8). In this task, we disguise a dependency conflict problem within a dense, unstructured narrative. The agent must look past domain-specific jargon and narrative flow to perform unstructured constraint extraction , ultimately identifying the underlying structural graph and any logical contradictions. For systemic conflict resolution , we instantiate this paradigm by describing a multi-year software setup timeline. By anchoring specific library installations to historical timestamps, the agent must synthesize the temporal context, retrieve historical compatibility matrices, and identify latent mutual exclusivity—such as version conflicts that render the described outcome physically impossible. 4.3 TYPE III: O RGANIZATION VIA SUBSTRUCTURE RE-ORDERING 

While Type I and Type II evaluate the discovery of existing connections and intersections, Type III tests the agent’s capacity to act as an structure processor—specifically, its ability to perform 

substructure re-ordering . In categorical terms, this is analogous to applying a sorting functor to construct a strictly ordered subcategory from chaotic data, and subsequently evaluating limits or tracing morphisms exclusively within that newly formed topological structure. Because commercial search engines inherently return flat, relevance-based results, Type III tasks force the agent to override this default topology. The agent must impose a strict mathematical ordering (e.g., ranking by floating-point values) on retrieved raw data, using this dynamically generated ordered list as an anchor for complex downstream reasoning. We formalize this capability through four distinct task paradigms. 

Rank-Conditioned Temporal Multi-Hop (Task 9). In this task, the agent must identify an entity based on a dynamic ordinal rank (e.g., the 5th highest-performing asset) at a specific discrete time 

T1. Once resolved, the agent executes a temporal jump to retrieve a highly volatile, state-dependent attribute of that entity at a distant historical time T2. This design deliberately breaks search index shortcuts . Because search engines index static names rather than daily dynamic ranks, the agent must autonomously construct the Top-N substructure, extract the k-th element, and only then formulate the secondary query. Furthermore, to enforce temporal state precision , T2 is strategically chosen to be a date immediately following a state transition (e.g., right after a manager resigns). This severely penalizes agents that hallucinate based on parametric priors or historical averages. 

Range-Bounded Topological Sorting (Task 10). In this task, the agent is required to filter a massive, cross-sectional dataset using a continuous floating-point interval [a, b ], dynamically generating a strict ordinal ranking of the resulting substructure from highest to lowest. This paradigm demands 

algorithmic exhaustiveness . Commercial search engines cannot natively execute granular queries like “return all entities with a growth rate between 7.3% and 7.6%.” Consequently, the agent must autonomously retrieve broad macro-level data tables, internalize the context, and act as a deterministic filtering and sorting algorithm. This serves as a rigorous stress test for the LLM’s inherent numerical reasoning and its ability to perform granular float-point comparison without omitting entities near the boundary conditions. 

Top-K Substructure Aggregation and Synthesis (Task 11). In this task, after retrieving and establishing a strictly ordered Top-K substructure based on a primary metric X (e.g., daily growth rate), the agent must compute macroscopic statistical invariants—such as the mean, variance, or 8mathematical range—over a parallel metric Y (e.g., net asset value) across those K entities. This requires Map-Reduce cognitive emulation , evaluating whether a dynamically re-ordered substructure can serve as a stable foundation for complex mathematical synthesis. It heavily probes context window integrity . Calculating an aggregate statistic forces an operation over the entire constructed subcategory, aggressively penalizing any data omission or alignment hallucination while the agent holds multiple distinct entities in its working memory. 

Rank-Anchored Time-Series Computation (Task 12). In this task, the agent identifies a target entity via its ordinal rank on a singular discrete timestamp Trank . It must then pivot its analytical lens to a continuous temporal window [Tstart , T end ] to compute a longitudinal time-series statistic (e.g., the volatility or range of cumulative values) for that specific entity. This necessitates orthogonal dimension shifting . The agent must seamlessly switch between two fundamentally different retrieval modalities: cross-sectional retrieval (evaluating all entities on a single day to establish the rank) and longitudinal retrieval (evaluating a single entity across multiple days). Ultimately, this demands 

complex working memory , ensuring the agent maintains rigorous schema isolation and does not conflate the metric used for initial ranking with the metric required for the downstream time-series computation. 4.4 TYPE IV: S TRUCTURE VALIDITY VIA THE YONEDA PROBE 

The ultimate hallmark of an autonomous Deep Research Agent lies not merely in its ability to retrieve existing information, but in its capacity to confidently prove non-existence. Rooted in the philosophy of falsification and the Yoneda Lemma (Mac Lane, 2013), Type IV tasks evaluate the agent’s ability to authenticate the ontological reality of an entity (or attribute) by exhaustively probing its external mappings (morphisms) within the knowledge space W. If an entity does not interact with the real world—yielding null retrieval morphisms—the agent must halt execution and refute the premise, rather than fabricating a hallucinated structure. Large language models inherently suffer from severe sycophancy and premise acceptance . If a user’s prompt implies the existence of a document or a data field, the agent’s parametric prior strongly biases it to fulfill the instruction via hallucination. To benchmark the agent’s zero-shot resistance to these presuppositional traps, we design three adversarial task paradigms. 

Schema-Level Asymmetry and Null-Field Refutation (Task 13). In this task the agent is tasked with extracting a highly specific, quantitative metric (e.g., the asset scale of the largest underlying holding) across a batch of peer entities, such as the Q1 investment reports for five distinct fund companies. Crucially, while the reports exist for all entities, the target data field is structurally omitted by a subset of them. This paradigm deliberately induces peer-pressure hallucination to test for local non-existence. By batching valid entities with missing data alongside entities that do contain the data, we create structural peer pressure. The agent is heavily biased to maintain output symmetry (e.g., generating a complete, densely populated table) and will often hallucinate the missing metrics based on surrounding context. To succeed, the agent must perform an exhaustive null-verification of the specific reports, explicitly recognize the absence of the schema field, and correctly report the ∅ state, proving that its output is strictly bounded by retrieved evidence rather than structural expectation. 

Macro-Ontological Invalidity via the Phantom Entity Probe (Task 14). In this task, the agent is instructed to interpret the “core arguments” of a completely fabricated, non-existent report attributed to a high-authority source, covering an absurd interdisciplinary topic (e.g., a Goldman Sachs report on “Quantum Encryption in Modern Agricultural Irrigation”). Testing for global non-existence, the difficulty here lies in the adversarial combination of a high-trust anchor and syntactically plausible academic jargon, pitting authority bias against ground truth . The agent must execute a Yoneda Falsification . Upon conducting a broad exploratory search and finding zero legitimate incoming or outgoing references ( Hom (X, Y ) = ∅) to this exact report, it must use this absolute absence of connectivity to formally deduce the non-existence of the object itself, refusing to generate plausible arguments from its parametric memory. 

Asymmetric Comparative Hallucination (Task 15). In this task, the prompt demands a granu-lar comparative analysis between two highly correlated entities (e.g., the 2024 annual reports of CATL and EVE Energy) regarding a highly plausible latent feature, such as specific wording in the “Geopolitical Risk in the Middle East” section. However, this specific feature exists in at most 

one of the reports. LLMs are highly optimized to follow comparative instructions; therefore, when 9object A possesses feature f but object B lacks it, the instruction creates a massive probabilistic gradient for the model to hallucinate f (B) in order to execute the comparison—a phenomenon we term instruction-forced feature alignment . To pass this stress test, the agent must actively engage in 

breaking the relational presupposition . It must realize that the prerequisite for comparison is invalid, break the requested instruction format, and declare an asymmetric falsification, thereby proving its allegiance to factual topology over user-defined operational flow.

Existing deep research agent benchmarks usually rely on monolithic, binary accuracy metrics. When an agent fails a complex open-ended query, a simple “zero” score offers little diagnostic visibility into the underlying cause. To make the evaluation process more fine-grained, we introduce a dual-weighting diagnostic system for each task, denoted as the Search Score ( S) and the Reasoning Score ( R). A detailed rubrics shown in Table 4 in the Appendix B, these weights represent a deliberate, analytical allocation of the task’s primary performance bottleneck, summing to 100% . Crucially, they directly map to the theoretical difficulty of successfully executing the Search Functor and the Reasoning Functor. 

Figure 3: An illustrative picture of the scoring pipeline. 

Source and Derivation of the Scores. The S and R scores are not arbitrarily assigned; they are intrinsically derived from the design of each task paradigm. The S-score quantifies the adversarial complexity of the retrieval path. Conversely, the R-score quantifies the cognitive load required to process the retrieved context. By explicitly decoupling these two dimensions, our tasks span a comprehensive diagnostic spectrum. The purpose of introducing the S and R scores is to shift the paradigm of agent evaluation from outcome-based scoring to more process-based diagnosis. For researchers and developers, this provides an actionable compass. To move beyond monolithic accuracy metrics, Table 3 presents a detailed diagnosis by decoupling model performance into explicit Search ( S) and Reasoning ( R) scores. The results clearly validate this diagnostic framework by revealing distinct capability bottlenecks and a stark performance variance, particularly within the Deep Research paradigm. While specialized models demonstrate isolated strengths—with Qwen3 w/Reasoning leading cognitive management ( R-score 12.8) and Doubao w/Search leading retrieval navigation ( S-score 4.4)—Deep Research models exhibit highly heterogeneous results. Some deep research models exhibit suppressed scores across both dimensions, failing to organically synthesize the Search and Reasoning Functors in autonomous loops. However, Grok Deep Research drastically breaks this mold, achieving an unprecedented S-score of 11.8 and an R-score of 21.6. This fine-grained breakdown empirically confirms that while the deep research pipeline may prone to compromised retrieval trajectories and diminished reasoning capacity, top-tier implementations can overcome these architectural bottlenecks and truly make the agent loop helpful.
